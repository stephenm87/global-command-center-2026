Lu Xun

The Intellectual Soul · 周树人 (1881–1936)
Lu Xun, Shanghai, c. 1930 · Photograph: Public Domain via Wikimedia Commons

Born Zhou Shuren in Shaoxing, Zhejiang province, Lu Xun initially studied medicine in Japan before making a fateful decision that would reshape modern Chinese literature. Witnessing the apathy of Chinese spectators watching footage of their own countrymen being executed, he concluded that China's sickness was not physical but spiritual — the nation needed writers, not doctors. Returning to China, he became the sharpest voice of the New Culture Movement, wielding his pen against what he called the "cannibalistic" traditions of Confucian society. His 1918 short story Diary of a Madman, the first major work of fiction written in vernacular Chinese rather than classical literary language, scandalized the intellectual establishment with its allegory of a society that devours its own people. During the chaos of the Warlord Era, Lu Xun relocated from Beijing to Shanghai, where he joined the League of Left-Wing Writers and championed the modern woodcut movement as a tool for social awakening. He wrote with a relentless urgency, declaring that China was a "sleeping nation" that must be roused before it was too late. His collected stories in Nahan ("Outcry") remain a foundational text for understanding the intellectual ferment that swept China between the fall of the Qing Dynasty and the rise of the Communist Party. Lu Xun died in Shanghai in 1936, leaving behind a legacy as the conscience of modern China.

Ding Ling

The New Woman · 丁玲 (1904–1986)
Ding Ling, c. 1930s · Photograph: Public Domain via Wikimedia Commons

Ding Ling arrived in Shanghai in 1920 as a young woman fleeing an arranged marriage, carrying only the fierce independence her mother — a rare female educator in Hunan province — had instilled in her. Within the literary salons of the city's French Concession, she found a voice that shocked Republican China. Her 1928 novella Miss Sophia's Diary was revolutionary not merely for its literary quality, but for its candid exploration of a young woman's desires, frustrations, and interior life — subjects that Chinese fiction had never before dared to address so directly. The story made her an overnight sensation and a symbol of the "New Woman" emerging from the wreckage of Confucian patriarchy. But Shanghai's glamour concealed brutal politics. In 1931, her husband, the poet Hu Yepin, was executed by Kuomintang secret police alongside twenty-three other leftist intellectuals. His death radicalized her entirely. She joined the Chinese Communist Party in 1932 and eventually made the dangerous journey to the revolutionary base at Yan'an, trading the literary salons for cave dwellings. Even there, she remained defiant — her 1942 essay criticizing the Party's treatment of women drew the wrath of Mao Zedong himself. She was persecuted, imprisoned during the Cultural Revolution, and not rehabilitated until 1979. Her journey from Shanghai rebel to Yan'an revolutionary embodies the radicalization of an entire generation who felt the Republic had failed them.

Dr. Lin Qiaozhi

The Healer · 林巧稚 (1901–1983)
Dr. Lin Qiaozhi, Peking Union Medical College, 1934 · Photograph: Public Domain via Wikimedia Commons

In 1929, a young woman from Xiamen became the first Chinese female graduate of the Peking Union Medical College — the most prestigious medical institution in Asia, founded with Rockefeller money and staffed by American physicians. Lin Qiaozhi chose to specialize in obstetrics and gynecology at a time when the vast majority of Chinese women gave birth without any medical assistance, attended only by midwives following centuries-old traditions. By 1940, she had risen to become the first Chinese woman to direct a department at PUMC Hospital, a feat of quiet determination in an era when women were still largely excluded from professional life. When the Pacific War erupted in 1941 and Japanese forces seized PUMC, closing the hospital, most foreign staff evacuated. Lin Qiaozhi stayed. She opened a private clinic in a Beijing hutong — a narrow alley of traditional courtyard houses — and continued treating patients, many of them too poor to pay. She delivered babies by candlelight during power blackouts, transforming her modest practice into a lifeline for the city's most vulnerable. Over the course of her career, she delivered more than 50,000 babies, earning the title "Mother of Ten Thousand Babies." In 1959, she became the sole female academician elected to the Chinese Academy of Sciences. Her life was a quiet rebellion against instability — while warlords, revolutionaries, and invaders fought over China's future, she focused on ensuring the survival of the next generation.

Charlie Soong

The Financier · 宋嘉澍 (1861–1918)
Charles Jones Soong, Vanderbilt University, c. 1882 · Photograph: Public Domain via Wikimedia Commons

Charles Jones Soong — born Han Jiao Zhun on the island of Hainan — was a stowaway, a convert, a missionary, a publisher, and ultimately the man who bankrolled Sun Yat-sen's revolution. Adopted at seventeen, he was sent to America, where he worked in a tea shop in Boston before running away to join the U.S. Revenue Marine. A chance encounter led to his conversion to Christianity and his enrollment at Trinity College in North Carolina (now Duke University) in 1881, making him the institution's first international student. After studying theology at Vanderbilt, he returned to China in 1886 as a Methodist missionary, but quickly realized that faith and commerce were more powerful together. In Shanghai, he built a printing empire, producing inexpensive vernacular Chinese Bibles that made him wealthy and influential. When he met Sun Yat-sen in 1894, the two men — both Western-educated, both dreaming of a modern China freed from Qing dynasty rule — became inseparable allies. Soong was appointed treasurer of the Revolutionary Alliance in 1906, secretly channeling his business profits into funding uprisings, printing political tracts, and smuggling arms. He bridged East and West in a way few others could, embodying the complexity of the new merchant class that sought to modernize China through capital and faith. His six children, all educated in the United States, went on to marry into the highest echelons of Chinese power — forming the Soong dynasty that shaped the nation's destiny for decades.

Lao She

The Everyman · 老舍 (1899–1966)
Lao She, Beijing, c. 1934 · Photograph: Public Domain via Wikimedia Commons

Shu Qingchun — known to the world as Lao She — was born into a poor Manchu family in Beijing in 1899, the year before his father, a palace guard, was killed during the Boxer Rebellion fighting foreign invaders. Growing up in the narrow hutongs of the old imperial capital, he absorbed the rhythms of street life — the cries of vendors, the clatter of rickshaw wheels on cobblestones, the language of people who lived and died without ever appearing in history books. After studying at Peking Normal School, he traveled to London in 1924 to teach Mandarin at the School of Oriental Studies, where he discovered Dickens and recognized a kindred spirit: a writer who made the struggles of the urban poor into great literature. Returning to China in 1930, Lao She poured his memories of Beijing's streets into his masterpiece, Rickshaw Boy (1936). The novel follows Xiangzi, an honest and hardworking rickshaw puller whose dreams are systematically crushed by a corrupt society — a fate that mirrored the experience of millions. Unlike Lu Xun's intellectual fury, Lao She wrote with heartbreaking tenderness for the "little people," revealing that for Beijing's urban poor, the Republic was a place of cultural beauty but devastating economic cruelty. His later play Teahouse (1957) chronicled fifty years of Chinese history through the conversations of ordinary people. Tragically, Lao She was persecuted during the Cultural Revolution and died in 1966 under disputed circumstances.
